Medieval Tithings - Catching a criminal

1000-1066 Anglo-Saxon England

Crime and Punishment during the Anglo-Saxon period

Law Enforcement


Community for the Anglo-Saxons was important especially for the reason that they did not have an established police force, this meant that the Saxons would have to rely on the community to deal with criminals. The Saxons' strategy for law enforcement was to introduce 'Tithings', these were small groups of 10 men that were collectively responsible for each other and the capture of criminals.The Church also played a significant role in law enforcement, it help in determining the accused as guilty or not, the Church had an overarching amount of power in society at the time and specifically with the Trials by Ordeal, these were not punishments but instead a way for the community to determine one's innocence.

How Criminals were Caught

When a crime was committed a Hue and Cry was raised and a group of Tithings is sent out to capture the culprit - soon after the criminal is captured the local community jury will be formed, mainly consisting of men from the village who knew both the accuser and the accused. The accuser and the accused would give their version of events and it was up to the jury to decide who was telling the truth. If there was no clear evidence such as an eyewitness to the crime, the jury decided guilt or innocence based on their knowledge of the people concerned. If the jury felt that the accuser was more honest than the accused, they would swear an oath that the accused was guilty. This oath taking was called compurgation.

However, if the jury could not come to an agreement, a Trial by Ordeal would be carried out, and if found guilty they would be punished by a court, and in most cases the most common method of punishment was Wergild.

Trials by Ordeal

There are four main types of Trial by Ordeal:

Trial Description
Trial by Hot Iron The accused had to carry a red-hot bar of iron and walk 9ft (3m). If the wound healed cleanly within three days, they were innocent. But if it festered, guilty.
Trial by Hot Water A large kettle of water would be heated to the boiling point and a ring or jewel placed at the bottom. The accused person reached in to grab the object; if he seized it and removed his arm without injury, the accused was deemed innocent.
Trial by Cold Water In a trial by cold water, the accused would be tied, thrown into a river, and found innocent if she sank, guilty if she floated.
Trial by Blessed Bread An accused priest would be required to take the wafer of communion. If they were guilty, the accused clergy member was expected to choke on the sacrament, and if innocent they would swallow it without incident.

William's New Laws

Not long after William's claim to the English Throne, did he and his men start a castle building project - William utilised Saxon peasants to construct these fortresses for Norman lords. These castles represented power and authority, as well as being used to keep an eye on people.

The feudal system had been used in France by the Normans from the time they first settled there in about 900AD. It was a simple, but effective system, where all land was owned by the King. One quarter was kept by the King as his personal property, some was given to the church and the rest was leased out under strict controls, this feudal system was introduced to England following the invasion. This system continued to fortify William's control over england by providing him, theoretically, absolute power.

Proceeding the establishment of new laws, the Murdrum fine, a derivative of the Werglid, but was used in the situation were a Norman was murdered by an Anglo-Saxon, this meant that if the killer wasn't caught the murdrum would be issued to the hundred where the body was found.

William declared that large stretches of the English countryside to be 'Royal Forests' which he and the wealthy would use for hunting. Land which had been common land was now controlled by the king. Although for peasants it was illegal to hunt, carry weapons or cut down trees - some of the harshest punishment were for breaking these Forest Laws.

Any man aged 14 and over, who tried to avoid trial and punishment by running away from his community was declared an outlaw. Women were said to be 'waived'. These outlaws or waived women lost the protection of the law. They could be killed without any legal consequences

Fines were now used to deal with lesser crimes, although the money no longer went to the victims family, instead it went toward the King's treasury.

William I set up the Church Courts to deal with moral crimes, strengthening the importance of the church, its strength also increased along with the more common use of oaths and trial by ordeal as the technique of determining guilt or innocence.

Norman-French became the the official language of court proceedings and records were in Latin.
