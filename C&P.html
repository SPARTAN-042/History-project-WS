Crime and Punishment during the Anglo-Saxon period

Law Enforcement

Community for the Anglo-Saxons was important especially for the reason that they did not have an established police force, this ment that the Saxons would have to rely on the community to deal with criminals. The Saxons' strategy for law enforcement was to introduce 'Tithings', these were small groups of 10 men that were collectively responsible for each other and the capture of criminals. The Church also played a significant role in law enforcement, it help in determining the accused as guilty or not, the Church had an overarching amount of power in society at the time and specifically with the Trials by Ordeal, these were not punishments but instead a way for the community to determine one's innocence.

How Criminals are Caught

Caption

When a crime was commited a Hue and Cry was raised and a group of Tithings is sent out to capture the culprit - soon after the criminal is captured the local community jury will be formed, mainly consisting of of men from the village who knew both the accuser and the accused. The accuser and the accused would give their version of events and it was up to the jury to decide who was telling the truth. If there was no clear evidence such as an eyewitness to the crime, the jury decided guilt or innocence based on their knowledge of the people concerned. If the jury felt that the accuser was more honest than the accused, they would swear an oath that the accused was guilty. This oath taking was called compurgation.
